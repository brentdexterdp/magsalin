Adjectives

Adjectives describe nouns and pronouns. Adjectives can describe kind, quantity, quality, or condition. In Filipino, adjectives are called pang-uri.

In Filipino, adjectives fall into two categories: panlarawan [qualitative] and pamilang [quantitative].

Pang-uring Panlarawan - Qualitative Adjectives

Qualitative adjectives describe the qualities of a noun or pronoun. These adjectives are the most common type of adjectives, and include the appearance, shape, size, color, and descriptive qualities of a noun or pronoun. Here are a few examples:

  • malaki - big
  • malinis - clean
  • maganda - beautiful
  • magaspang - rough
  • magulo - messy

Pang-uring Pamilang - Quantitative Adjectives

Quantitative adjectives describe the quantity of a noun or pronoun. These adjectives include numbers, fractions, and other words that describe quantity. Here are a few examples:

  • isa - one
  • marami - many
  • kaunti - few
  • kalahati - half
  • buo - whole

The most basic quantitative adjectives are numbers. It's important to know how to write numbers in Filipino because they are used in everyday life. Let's start with the 10 basic numbers:

  • isa - one
  • dalawa - two
  • tatlo - three
  • apat - four
  • lima - five
  • anim - six
  • pito - seven
  • walo - eight
  • siyam - nine
  • sampu - ten

To write numbers 11 to 19, follow the steps below:

  1. Write "labin" or "labing" to signify the tens part. Use "labin" if the ones part starts with a consonant. Otherwise, use "labing".
  2. Concatenate the translation of the ones part to the tens part.

For example, the number 11 translates to labingisa and the number 12 translates to labingdalawa. For your reference, here's the translation of numbers 11 to 19:

  • labingisa - eleven
  • labindalawa - twelve
  • labintatlo - thirteen
  • labingapat - fourteen
  • labinlima - fifteen
  • labinganim - sixteen
  • labinpito - seventeen
  • labinwalo - eighteen
  • labinsiyam - nineteen

To translate numbers 20 to 99, we first need to know the translation of 20, 30, 40, etc. See the table below:

  • dalawampu - twenty
  • tatlumpu - thirty
  • apatnapu - forty
  • limampu - fifty
  • animnapu - sixty
  • pitumpu - seventy
  • walumpu - eighty
  • siyamnapu - ninety

If the ones part is 0, you can use the table above for the translation. For example, the number 20 has 0 for the ones part. Therefore, it is translated as dalawmpu. On the other hand, if the ones part is not zero, you should follow the steps below:

  1. Write the tens part as described by the table above.
  2. Concatenate 't to the tens part. This means "and" in English.
  3. Add the ones part translation.

For example, the number 35 is translated as tatlumpu't lima and the number 96 is translated as siyamnapu't anim.

The process of translating numbers bigger than 99 is more general. In this section, we will only cover up to the thousands part. You first need to know the translation of "hundred" and "thousand" in Filipino:

  • daan - hundred
  • libo - thousand

To translate numbers with a hundreds part, follow the steps below:

  1. Omit the tens and ones part. For example, the number 178 becomes "1".
  2. Translate the number as if it's a ones part. The "1" in 178 becomes isa.
  3. Concatenate "ng" if the translation ends with a vowel. Otherwise, add "na". Afterwards, add the word "daan". For example, "1" becomes isang daan while "4" becomes apat na daan.
  4. If there's a tens and ones part, add the word "at". This means "and" in English.
  5. If there's a tens and ones part, add the translation. Use the rules discussed above for the ones and tens part.

For example, the number 178 translates to isang daan at pitumpu't walo and the number 456 translates to apat na daan at limampu't anim.

Translating numbers with a thousands part is similar to the hundreds case. Follow the steps below:

  1. Omit the hundreds, tens, and ones part. For example, the number 178593 becomes "178".
  2. Translate the number using the rules for the hundreds, tens, and ones part. The "178" in 178593 becomes isang daan at pitumpu't walo.
  3. Concatenate "ng" if the translation ends with a vowel. Otherwise, add "na". Afterwards, add the word "libo". For example, "178" becomes isang daan at pitumpu't walong libo while "456" becomes apat na daan at limampu't anim na libo.
  4. If there's a hundreds, tens, and ones part, concatenate 't. This means "and" in English.
  5. If there's a hundreds, tens, and ones part, add the translation. Use the rules discussed above for the hundreds, tens, and ones part.

For example, the number 178593 translates to isang daan at pitumpu't walong libo't limang daan at siyamnapu't tatlo and the number 456845 translates to apat na daan at limampu't anim na libo't walong daan at apatnapu't lima.

Once you know how to write numbers in Filipino, you can use them to describe nouns. You just need to concatenate "ng" if the translation ends with a vowel or add "na" if the translations ends with a consonant.

For example, "178 chickens" translates to isang daan at pitumpu't walong manok while "456 chickens" translates to apat na daan at limampu't anim na manok.
